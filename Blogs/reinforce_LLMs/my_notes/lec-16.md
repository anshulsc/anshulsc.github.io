## 1. Introduction and Historical Context

In the development of modern Large Language Models (LLMs), Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) serves as a primary methodology for aligning model behaviors with human preferences. At the core of practical RLHF implementations is **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)**. 

To understand why PPO became the standard alignment algorithm, we must trace its evolution from two preceding classes of reinforcement learning methods:
1. **Vanilla Policy Gradient (VPG) methods:** Simple first-order optimization techniques that suffer from high variance and destructive step-size updates.
2. **Trust Region methods (e.g., TRPO):** Mathematically rigorous algorithms that guarantee stable policy updates but are computationally expensive due to the requirement of second-order derivative calculations.

PPO was developed in 2017 to combine the computational simplicity of first-order gradient methods with the stability of trust-region optimization.

## 2. First Principles: Vanilla Policy Gradient (VPG)

### 2.1 The Objective and Gradient Formulation
In a reinforcement learning framework, we define a policy $\pi_\theta(a|s)$, which represents the probability of taking action $a$ given state $s$, parameterized by $\theta$. The goal is to maximize the expected cumulative reward.

For vanilla policy gradient methods, the gradient of the performance measure $J(\theta)$ is given by the Policy Gradient Theorem:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta} \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) \right]$$

Where:
*   $\theta$ represents the parameters of the policy network.
*   $\mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}$ is the expectation over state-action trajectories generated by the policy $\pi_\theta$.
*   $\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t)$ is the score function, indicating the direction in parameter space that increases the probability of choosing action $A_t$ in state $S_t$.
*   $\hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t)$ is the estimated advantage function at time step $t$.

### 2.2 Intuition of the Advantage Function $\hat{A}_t$
The advantage function measures how much better (or worse) a specific action $A_t$ is compared to the average behavior of the current policy in state $S_t$:

$$\hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) = Q(S_t, A_t) - V(S_t)$$

Where $Q(S_t, A_t)$ is the expected return of taking action $A_t$, and $V(S_t)$ is the baseline expected return for state $S_t$.
*   **If $\hat{A}_t > 0$ (Positive Advantage):** The action performed better than average. We want to increase the probability of this action by moving the parameters $\theta$ in the direction of the gradient $\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t)$.
*   **If $\hat{A}_t < 0$ (Negative Advantage):** The action performed worse than average. We want to decrease the probability of this action.

### 2.3 Visualizing the Parameter Space
Imagine a two-dimensional parameter space defined by $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$, where the vertical axis represents the performance measure $J(\theta)$. This creates a performance surface or "mountain."

```
        Performance J(θ)
             ▲
            / \     ◄--- Optimize to reach the peak
           /   \
          /     \
   ──────▲───────▲──────► Parameter Space (θ1, θ2)
```

The objective is to climb this mountain using gradient ascent. The update rule is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$$

where $\alpha$ is the step size (learning rate).

### 2.4 The Corresponding Objective Function
The objective function whose gradient with respect to $\theta$ equals the policy gradient estimator is written as:

$$L^{PG}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) \right]$$

When we take the derivative of $L^{PG}(\theta)$, we retrieve the policy gradient:

$$\nabla_\theta L^{PG}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) \right]$$

### 2.5 Limitations of $L^{PG}$
In practice, optimizing $L^{PG}(\theta)$ directly using multiple steps of gradient ascent on the same batch of data leads to unstable behavior. The log-probability $\log \pi_\theta(A_t | S_t)$ can be driven to extreme values, causing the policy to collapse or change too drastically, moving the model into a region of parameter space where performance is poor.

## 3. Trust Region Methods

To address the instability of vanilla policy gradients, trust region methods introduce constraints to ensure that the updated policy does not deviate too far from the old policy.

### 3.1 The Surrogate Objective
Rather than using log-probabilities, trust region methods optimize a surrogate objective using the probability ratio:

$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(A_t | S_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(A_t | S_t)}$$

The surrogate objective is defined as:

$$L^{CPI}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) \right]$$

*Note: $CPI$ stands for Conservative Policy Iteration.*

### 3.2 The Constrained Optimization Problem (TRPO)
In Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO), the surrogate objective is maximized subject to a constraint on the size of the policy update, measured by the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence:

$$\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_t \left[ r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) \right]$$

$$\text{subject to } \mathbb{E}_t \left[ D_{KL} \left( \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot | S_t) \,||\, \pi_\theta(\cdot | S_t) \right) \right] \le \delta$$

Where $\delta$ is a hyperparameter determining the maximum allowed step size in policy space.

Alternatively, this constrained problem can be formulated as an unconstrained optimization problem using a Lagrange multiplier (penalty method):

$$\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_t \left[ r_t(\theta) \hat{A}_t(S_t, A_t) - \beta D_{KL} \left( \pi_{\theta_{old}}(\cdot | S_t) \,||\, \pi_\theta(\cdot | S_t) \right) \right]$$

### 3.3 The Bottleneck of TRPO
While TRPO is theoretically stable, solving this constrained problem requires computing the Fisher Information Matrix (FIM) to approximate the KL divergence constraint. This involves:
1. Computing second-order derivatives.
2. Inverting a large matrix, which scales quadratically or cubically with the number of parameters.

For modern deep neural networks and large language models with billions of parameters, inverting this matrix is computationally impractical.

## 4. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)

PPO retains the benefits of trust region methods (avoiding destructively large updates) while relying only on first-order derivatives, making it highly scalable. It achieves this through the **Clipped Surrogate Objective**.

### 4.1 The Clipped Surrogate Objective Formulation
PPO defines the objective function as:

$$L^{CLIP}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ \min \left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t, \, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right) \right]$$

Where:
*   $r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(A_t | S_t)}{\pi_{\theta_{old}}(A_t | S_t)}$
*   $\epsilon$ is a hyperparameter (typically set to $0.1$ or $0.2$).
*   The $\text{clip}$ operator restricts the ratio $r_t(\theta)$ to the interval $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$.

To understand why this formulation works, we analyze it under two distinct scenarios: positive advantage and negative advantage.

---

### 4.2 Case 1: Positive Advantage ($\hat{A}_t > 0$)

When the advantage is positive, the chosen action yielded a better-than-average return. We want to increase the probability of this action, which means we want $r_t(\theta) > 1$.

In this case, the objective inside the expectation simplifies to:

$$\min \left( r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t, \, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t \right)$$

Because $\hat{A}_t > 0$, we can factor it out of the $\min$ operation:

$$\min \left( r_t(\theta), \, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \right) \hat{A}_t$$

```
   Clipped Objective (At > 0)
        ▲
        │                / (Unclipped: rt * At)
        │               /
   (1+ε)│  ┌───────────▀   ◄--- Clipped Region (slope = 0)
        │  │           
        │ / 
        │/
   ─────┼──────┬───────┬──────► Ratio rt(θ)
        │      1      1+ε
```

#### Behavior Analysis:
*   **When $r_t(\theta) \le 1+\epsilon$:** The ratio is within the allowed limit. The $\text{clip}$ function returns $r_t(\theta)$. The minimum of $r_t(\theta)$ and $r_t(\theta)$ is simply $r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t$. The objective increases linearly with $r_t(\theta)$, encouraging the policy to make this action more likely.
*   **When $r_t(\theta) > 1+\epsilon$:** The policy has updated significantly, making the action much more likely than before. The $\text{clip}$ function limits the value to $1+\epsilon$. The term becomes $\min(r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t, (1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t)$. Since $r_t > 1+\epsilon$, the minimum operation selects the clipped value:

$$(1+\epsilon)\hat{A}_t$$

*   **Resulting Intuition:** Once the policy has increased the action probability beyond the threshold $1+\epsilon$, further updates on this data sample yield no additional gradient. This prevents the policy from becoming overly greedy and destabilizing the model.

---

### 4.3 Case 2: Negative Advantage ($\hat{A}_t < 0$)

When the advantage is negative, the chosen action performed worse than average. We want to decrease the probability of this action, meaning we want $r_t(\theta) < 1$.

Let $\hat{A}_t = -|\hat{A}_t|$ to make the negative sign explicit. The objective is:

$$\min \left( -r_t(\theta)|\hat{A}_t|, \, -\text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon)|\hat{A}_t| \right)$$

Factor out the negative advantage $-|\hat{A}_t|$:

$$-\max \left( r_t(\theta), \, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) \right) |\hat{A}_t|$$

```
   Clipped Objective (At < 0)
        ▲
        │      1-ε     1
   ─────┼──────┼───────┼──────► Ratio rt(θ)
        │     /│
        │    / │
   (1-ε)│  ┌▀  │           ◄--- Clipped Region (rt < 1-ε)
        │  │   │
        │  │   │
        ▼  ▼   │  
               \   ◄--- Unclipped penalty continues when rt > 1
                \
```

#### Behavior Analysis:
*   **When $r_t(\theta) \ge 1-\epsilon$:** The ratio is within the safe bounds (or is greater than 1). The maximum of $r_t(\theta)$ and $\text{clip}(r_t(\theta))$ is $r_t(\theta)$. The overall term remains $r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t$. The policy is penalized proportionally to $r_t(\theta)$, driving the probability of this bad action down.
*   **When $r_t(\theta) < 1-\epsilon$:** The policy has successfully decreased the probability of the bad action. The clipped term is $(1-\epsilon)\hat{A}_t$. Because $r_t < 1-\epsilon$, the term $-r_t|\hat{A}_t|$ is less negative (closer to zero) than $-(1-\epsilon)|\hat{A}_t|$. The minimum operation selects the more negative value:

$$(1-\epsilon)\hat{A}_t$$

*   **When $r_t(\theta) > 1+\epsilon$:** If the policy accidentally updates in the wrong direction—making a bad action *more* likely—the ratio $r_t(\theta)$ is high. The minimum of $-r_t(\theta)|\hat{A}_t|$ and $-(1+\epsilon)|\hat{A}_t|$ is:

$$-r_t(\theta)|\hat{A}_t|$$

*   **Resulting Intuition:** For negative advantages, we do *not* clip when the ratio $r_t(\theta)$ grows large. If the model makes a bad action much more likely, we want to apply the full, unclipped penalty to quickly correct this error. We only clip when the ratio goes below $1-\epsilon$, preventing the model from over-penalizing an already suppressed action.

---

### 4.4 Summary of Clipping Behavior
The dual-graph representation below shows how the clipped objective function behaves depending on the sign of the advantage:

```
                  At > 0                                At < 0
             L^CLIP                                L^CLIP
               ▲                                     ▲
               │       /                             │     /

               │     /                               │   /
               │    /                                │  /
```

## 5. The Complete PPO Objective

When implementing PPO in practice using neural networks (including actor-critic architectures and LLM alignment setups), we optimize a unified multi-task loss function. This function contains three distinct terms: the policy loss, the value function loss, and an entropy bonus.

### 5.1 Value Function Loss ($L^{VF}$)
The advantage estimator $\hat{A}_t$ is computed using value function estimates:

$$\hat{A}_t = R_t - V_\phi(S_t)$$

where $V_\phi(S_t)$ is the state-value predicted by the network parameterized by $\phi$. If we share parameters between the policy network and the value network (as is common to improve feature representation), we must include the value function optimization error directly in our objective:

$$L^{VF}(\theta) = \left( V_\theta(S_t) - V_t^{target} \right)^2$$

Optimizing this term ensures the baseline remains accurate, which in turn reduces the variance of the advantage estimates.

### 5.2 Entropy Bonus ($S$)
To prevent premature convergence and encourage exploration, an entropy bonus $S[\pi_\theta](S_t)$ is added to the objective:

$$S[\pi_\theta](S_t) = -\sum_a \pi_\theta(a | S_t) \log \pi_\theta(a | S_t)$$

Maximizing entropy ensures the policy maintains some level of randomness, preventing it from collapsing into a deterministic, sub-optimal strategy too early in training.

### 5.3 Combined Objective Function
The complete objective maximized by PPO during training is:

$$L^{PPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t \left[ L^{CLIP}(\theta) - c_1 L^{VF}(\theta) + c_2 S[\pi_\theta](S_t) \right]$$

Where:
*   $c_1$ and $c_2$ are hyperparameter coefficients.
*   $c_1$ typically balances the value function loss (often set to $0.5$ or $1.0$).
*   $c_2$ controls the strength of the entropy bonus (often set to a small value like $0.01$).

By using gradient ascent on $L^{PPO}(\theta)$, we update our policy parameters:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + \alpha \nabla_\theta L^{PPO}(\theta)$$

## 6. Summary of PPO in Large Language Models

| Metric / Aspect | Vanilla Policy Gradient (VPG) | Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) | Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Optimization Order** | First-Order | Second-Order | **First-Order** |
| **Computation Cost** | Low | High (requires FIM inversion) | **Low** |
| **Update Stability** | Low (susceptible to collapse) | High (enforced by KL constraint) | **High (enforced by Clipping)** |
| **Implementation Ease**| Easy | Hard | **Easy** |

### Modern Significance
PPO is widely utilized in the post-training alignment phase of large language models. For example, the initial alignment of models like ChatGPT relied on PPO to optimize the output of the model against a learned reward model. By using the clipped surrogate objective, PPO ensures the language model does not diverge too far from its initial pre-trained state-distribution, preserving general language capabilities while adapting to human preferences.
