## 1. Introduction & Historical Context of RL in LLMs

This lecture explores the integration of Reinforcement Learning (RL) within Large Language Models (LLMs), with a focus on **Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)**. 

Historically, RL was primarily applied to LLMs for **Alignment** with human preferencesвҖ”a process known as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). Algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) were used to fine-tune pre-trained models to be more helpful, honest, and harmless based on human evaluations. However, these early alignment frameworks did not focus on teaching the model step-by-step reasoning or mathematical problem-solving.

```
       [Pre-trained LLM]
               в”Ӯ
               в–ј
   [Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)]
               в”Ӯ
      в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”ҙв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”җ
      в–ј                 в–ј
  [Classic RLHF]   [Reasoning RL]
  (Preference)     (Step-by-Step)
  - PPO            - GRPO (DeepSeek-R1)
  - DPO            - Process Supervision
```

With the release of architectures like DeepSeek-R1 in early 2025, a paradigm shift occurred. Researchers demonstrated that pure reinforcement learning, combined with specialized policy optimization algorithms, can induce complex step-by-step **thinking patterns** and **reasoning capabilities** in LLMs without requiring massive amounts of supervised reasoning data. 

GRPO serves as a cornerstone of this transition by eliminating the massive memory and compute overhead associated with classic Actor-Critic RL frameworks.

To apply reinforcement learning to language models, we must cast the text-generation process into the standard **Markov Decision Process (MDP)** or Agent-Environment framework.

### The MDP Formulation for LLMs
* **Agent**: The Large Language Model itself, parameterized by weights $\theta$ (denoted as the policy $\pi_\theta$).
* **Environment**: The context window containing the prompt and the history of generated tokens.
* **State ($s_t$)**: The sequence of tokens available to the model at step $t$. This includes the initial prompt $x$ and all previously generated tokens $y_{<t}$:
  $$s_t = (x, y_1, y_2, \dots, y_{t-1})$$
* **Action ($a_t$)**: The generation of the next token $y_t$ from the vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$:
  $$a_t = y_t \in \mathcal{V}$$
* **Transition Dynamics**: Deterministic token concatenation. When the agent takes action $a_t = y_t$ in state $s_t$, the environment transitions to the next state:
  $$s_{t+1} = s_t \cup \{y_t\} = (x, y_1, \dots, y_t)$$
* **Policy ($\pi_\theta(a|s)$)**: The probability distribution over the vocabulary $\mathcal{V}$ given the current state:
  $$\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t) = P(y_t | x, y_1, \dots, y_{t-1})$$

### Concrete Trace of a Reasoning Loop
Consider the following prompt $x$ and completion $y$:

* **Prompt ($x$)**: *"Roger has 5 tennis balls. He buys 2 more cans of tennis balls. Each can has 3 tennis balls. How many tennis balls does he have now?"*
* **Target Answer ($y$)**: *"Roger has 11 tennis balls."*

Let's break down how this is generated step-by-step:

1. **Step $t=0$**:
   * State $s_0 = x$ (the prompt).
   * Action $a_0 = y_1 = \text{"Roger"}$, sampled from the distribution $\pi_\theta(y_1 | x)$.
2. **Step $t=1$**:
   * State $s_1 = (x, \text{"Roger"})$.
   * Action $a_1 = y_2 = \text{"has"}$, sampled from $\pi_\theta(y_2 | x, \text{"Roger"})$.
3. **Step $t=2$**:
   * State $s_2 = (x, \text{"Roger"}, \text{"has"})$.
   * Action $a_2 = y_3 = \text{"11"}$, sampled from $\pi_\theta(y_3 | x, \text{"Roger"}, \text{"has"})$.
4. This continues until the end-of-sequence token (e.g., `<|end_of_text|>`) is generated, signaling the completion of the episode.

## 3. Mathematical Foundations of Policy Gradients in LLMs

### Formal Notations
* **Prompt ($x$)**: An input sequence of $T_x$ tokens sampled from a query distribution:
  $$x = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_{T_x}\} \sim \mathcal{D}_x$$
* **Completion ($y$)**: An output sequence of $T_y$ tokens generated by the LLM:
  $$y = \{y_1, y_2, \dots, y_{T_y}\}$$
* **Policy ($\pi_\theta$)**: The model parameters $\theta$ that define the auto-regressive probability of a completion sequence $y$ given prompt $x$:
  $$\pi_\theta(y | x) = \prod_{t=1}^{|y|} \pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})$$

### The Policy Gradient Formulation
In reinforcement learning, our goal is to maximize the expected reward of our policy:
$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} [R(x, y)]$$

Using the **Policy Gradient Theorem**, we compute the gradient of this objective with respect to the policy parameters $\theta$:
$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y | x) R(x, y) \right]$$

Since language models generate text auto-regressively, we can expand this to a token-level loss using the chain rule of probabilities:
$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) A_t \right]$$
where $A_t$ represents the **Advantage** of taking action $y_t$ at step $t$ compared to the default behavior.

---

### Step-by-Step Derivation of PPO for LLMs

To update policy parameters off-policy (using data collected from an older policy version $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$), we use **Importance Sampling**.

#### First Principles of Importance Sampling
Suppose we want to estimate the expectation of a function $f(y)$ where $y$ is drawn from a target distribution $p(y)$, but we only have samples from a proposal distribution $q(y)$:
$$\mathbb{E}_{y ~ p} [f(y)] = \sum_{y} p(y) f(y) = \sum_{y} q(y) \frac{p(y)}{q(y)} f(y) = \mathbb{E}_{y \sim q} \left[ \frac{p(y)}{q(y)} f(y) \right]$$

Applying this to our policy optimization objective allows us to rewrite the expected advantage under a new policy $\pi_\theta$ using samples collected from our old policy $\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}$:
$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(y | x)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y | x)} A(x, y) \right]$$

At the token level, we define the probability ratio $r_t(\theta)$ for step $t$:
$$r_t(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})}$$

The vanilla surrogate policy objective is:
$$L_{PG}(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \left[ r_t(\theta) A_t \right]$$

#### The Clipping Mechanism in PPO
If we optimize $L_{PG}(\theta)$ without constraints, the step size can be unsafely large, causing the policy to destabilize or experience "policy collapse." To prevent this, **Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)** clips the probability ratio $r_t(\theta)$ within a small interval $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ (where typically $\epsilon \approx 0.1$ or $0.2$).

```
           Surrogate Objective L^CLIP
                     в–І
                     в”Ӯ          / (Unclipped r_t * A_t for positive A_t)
                     в”Ӯ         /
                     в”Ӯ  в”Ңв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв–Җ (1+Оө)A_t
                     в”Ӯ  в”Ӯ     в–Җ
                     в”Ӯ  в”Ӯ     в–Җ  <-- Clipped Region
                     в”Ӯ  в”Ӯ     в–Җ
                     в”Ӯ  в–Җв–Җв–Җв–Җв–Җв–Җв–Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв–ә r_t
                     в”Ӯ (1-Оө)
                     в”Ӯ
                     в””в”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв–ә
```

The mathematical formulation of the PPO objective is:
$$L_{PPO}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta} \left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \min \left( r_t(\theta) A_t, \, \text{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_t \right) \right]$$

1. **Actor ($\pi_\theta$)**: The active LLM policy being optimized.
2. **Reference Model ($\pi_{\text{ref}}$)**: A frozen copy of the initial SFT model used to compute a KL divergence penalty, ensuring the active policy does not drift too far from natural language distributions.

---

### The Memory & Compute Bottleneck

While PPO works well, it is highly resource-intensive when scaled to LLMs due to the **Critic/Value Model**:

$$\text{Memory Overhead} \propto \text{Size}(\text{Actor}) + \text{Size}(\text{Critic}) + \text{Size}(\text{Reference}) + \text{Size}(\text{Reward})$$

* **Double Parameter Footprint**: The Critic must estimate a value for every generated token. Because learning value representations over raw text is highly complex, the Critic must be comparable in size to the Actor (e.g., if the Actor is a 70B parameter model, the Critic is typically also a 70B parameter model).
* **Optimizer States**: Training both the Actor and Critic simultaneously requires maintaining their respective optimizer states (e.g., Adamw states require 8 bytes per parameter), which often doubles the active GPU memory requirement.
* **Over-parameterization for Simple Baselines**: The primary role of the Critic is to provide a baseline $V(s)$ to calculate the advantage:
  $$A_t = Q(s, a) - V(s)$$
  Using an entire 70-billion-parameter neural network just to calculate a baseline for a given prompt is mathematically and computationally inefficient.

## 5. Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)

### First Principles of the Advantage Function

To understand how GRPO eliminates the Critic model, we look at the definition of **Advantage** in Reinforcement Learning:
$$A(s, a) = Q_\pi(s, a) - V_\pi(s)$$

* **$Q_\pi(s, a)$**: The state-action value function. It represents the expected cumulative reward if the agent takes action $a$ in state $s$, and thereafter follows policy $\pi$.
* **$V_\pi(s)$**: The state value function. It represents the expected cumulative reward if the agent follows policy $\pi$ from state $s$:
  $$V_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot|s)} [Q_\pi(s, a)]$$

Thus, the baseline $V_\pi(s)$ is simply the average expected reward of all possible actions the current policy could take from state $s$.

---

### Eliminating the Critic via Group Sampling

Instead of training a Critic network to approximate $V_\pi(s)$, GRPO estimates this baseline statistically by sampling a **group** of $G$ outputs for a single prompt $x$ using the current policy $\pi_\theta$.

1. For a prompt $x$, sample a group of $G$ outputs:
   $$\{y_1, y_2, \dots, y_G\} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|x)$$
2. Use the Reward Model (or a programmatic verifier) to compute a scalar reward $r_i$ for each completion $y_i$:
   $$r_i = R(x, y_i) \quad \forall i \in \{1, 2, \dots, G\}$$
3. Compute the sample mean $\mu$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ of the rewards for this group:
   $$\mu = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G r_i$$
   $$\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^G (r_i - \mu)^2 + \epsilon_0}$$
   *(where $\epsilon_0$ is a small constant like $10^{-8}$ added for numerical stability)*
4. The relative advantage $A_i$ for each completion $y_i$ in the group is calculated by standardizing the reward:
   $$A_i = \frac{r_i - \mu}{\sigma}$$

#### Intuition
By comparing the outputs within the sampled group, the average reward $\mu$ acts as a dynamic baseline. If a generated completion $y_i$ gets a higher reward than the group average ($r_i > \mu$), its advantage is positive, and the policy updates to make those tokens more likely. 

Conversely, if an output is worse than average ($r_i < \mu$), its advantage is negative, and the policy updates to decrease its probability.

This approach **completely eliminates the Critic/Value Model**, significantly reducing GPU memory usage and simplifying the training pipeline.

## 6. Outcome Supervision vs. Process Supervision

When applying GRPO to reasoning tasks, the structure of the reward signal determines how effectively the model learns.

### Outcome-Supervised RL (ORM)
In outcome-supervised learning, the reward model only evaluates the final output at the very end of the sequence.

```
 Roger starts with 5... в”Җв”Җв–ә He buys 2 more... в”Җв”Җв–ә Answer is 11. [Reward R = 1.0]
```

* **Limitation**: The model only receives feedback on whether its final answer was correct. It does not receive feedback on the correctness of its intermediate thoughts. If the model arrives at the correct answer through flawed logic, it still receives a positive reward, which can reinforce bad reasoning habits.

---

### Process-Supervised RL (PRM)
Process-supervised learning evaluates each intermediate step (or "thought") within the model's Chain of Thought (CoT).

```
 Roger starts with 5... в”Җв”Җв–ә He buys 2 more... в”Җв”Җв–ә 2 x 3 = 6... в”Җв”Җв–ә 5 + 6 = 11.
      [Reward R_1]             [Reward R_2]         [Reward R_3]     [Reward R_4]
```

To optimize reasoning steps using GRPO and process supervision, we sample multiple completions, identify the individual reasoning steps within each completion, and assign a reward to each step.

* **Cumulative Future Rewards**: For a reasoning step $k$ in completion $i$, the return is the sum of future rewards from that step onward:
  $$G_{k, i} = \sum_{j=k}^{K_i} \gamma^{j-k} R_{i, j}$$
  *(where $\gamma$ is a discount factor, and $K_i$ is the number of steps in completion $y_i$)*
* **Group Normalization at Step Level**: We normalize these cumulative returns across the group at equivalent step intervals to compute a step-level advantage $A_{t, i}$:
  $$A_{t, i} = \frac{G_{t, i} - \mu_t}{\sigma_t}$$

By reinforcing individual thoughts rather than just the final answer, process-supervised GRPO helps the model learn structured and reliable reasoning paths.

---

## 7. Derivation of the Complete GRPO Loss Function

We now derive the complete objective function for Group Relative Policy Optimization, incorporating group-level advantages and a KL-divergence constraint.

### 1. The Group Surrogate Loss Term
First, we average the standard PPO clipped surrogate objective over our sampled group of size $G$:

$$L_{\text{Surr}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min \left( r_{t, i}(\theta) A_{t, i}, \, \text{clip}(r_{t, i}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_{t, i} \right)$$

where:
* $G$ is the group size.
* $|y_i|$ is the sequence length of the $i$-th completion.
* $r_{t, i}(\theta) = \frac{\pi_\theta(y_{t, i} | x, y_{<t, i})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(y_{t, i} | x, y_{<t, i})}$ is the token-level probability ratio.
* $A_{t, i}$ is the standardized group relative advantage.

---

### 2. The KL Divergence Penalty
To prevent the active policy $\pi_\theta$ from drifting too far from the base reference model $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, we add a token-level Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence penalty.

#### Mathematical Definition of KL Divergence
$$D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) = \sum_{a \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_\theta(a | s) \log \left( \frac{\pi_\theta(a | s)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a | s)} \right)$$

In practice, we estimate this expectation using our active policy samples, which simplifies the token-level penalty to:
$$D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) \approx \log \left( \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})} \right) = \log \pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t}) - \log \pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})$$

Alternatively, we can use the unbiased estimator:
$$D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) = \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})} - \log \frac{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})} - 1$$

Using the direct ratio definition, the penalized surrogate objective is:

$$L_{\text{KL}}(\theta) = \beta \cdot \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, y \sim \pi_\theta} \left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \left( \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_t | x, y_{<t})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_t | x, y_{<t})} \right) \right]$$

where $\beta$ is a scaling hyperparameter controlling the strength of the constraint.

---

### 3. The Complete GRPO Objective Function
Combining the group surrogate loss and the KL divergence penalty, we arrive at the final GRPO objective maximized during training:

$$\bbox[15px,border:2px solid #5D5D5D]{L_{\text{GRPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D}_x, \, \{y_i\}_{i=1}^G \sim \pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \left( \frac{1}{|y_i|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y_i|} \min \left( r_{t, i}(\theta) A_{t, i}, \, \text{clip}(r_{t, i}(\theta), 1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon) A_{t, i} \right) \right) - \beta D_{KL}(\pi_\theta || \pi_{\text{ref}}) \right]}$$

## 8. Summary Comparison: PPO vs. GRPO

| Feature / Metric | Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) | Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Value Function / Critic** | Required (actively trained neural network) | **None** (statistically estimated from group samples) |
| **Active Models in Memory** | 4 (Actor, Critic, Reference, Reward) | **2** (Actor, Reference - Reward is called only for inference) |
| **Memory Footprint** | Extremely High | **Low to Moderate** |
| **Advantage Baseline** | State-value prediction $V_\phi(s)$ | Group mean reward $\mu$ of sampled completions |
| **Key Use Case** | Classic preference alignment (RLHF) | Step-by-step reasoning & math (DeepSeek-R1) |
