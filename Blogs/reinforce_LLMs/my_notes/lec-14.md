## 1. Introduction and Context

In modern Reinforcement Learning (RL) and its applications to Large Language Models (LLMs)—including alignment algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO, utilized in reasoning models such as DeepSeek)—the mathematical foundations of policy updates trace back directly to **Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO)** (Schulman et al., 2015). 

While modern algorithms modify the optimization constraints for computational efficiency, their structural objectives are derived from TRPO. This lecture covers the foundational theory of TRPO, building from first principles to derive how we compare policies, construct surrogate objectives, and establish trust regions to guarantee monotonic policy improvement.

---

## 2. Foundational Concepts

### 2.1. Policy Performance Measure ($\eta$)
To evaluate how well a policy $\pi$ performs, we define its expected discounted cumulative reward starting from an initial state $s_0$:

$$\eta(\pi) = v_\pi(s_0) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R(s_t) \right]$$

Where:
*   $\gamma \in [0, 1)$ is the discount factor.
*   $R(s_t)$ is the reward received at time step $t$.
*   $\tau = (s_0, a_0, s_1, a_1, \dots)$ represents a trajectory generated by the agent interacting with the environment using policy $\pi$.

---

### 2.2. The Advantage Function ($A_\pi$)
The Advantage Function, denoted as $A_\pi(s, a)$, measures how much better (or worse) a specific action $a$ is compared to the default behavior of the current policy $\pi$ in state $s$:

$$A_\pi(s, a) = Q_\pi(s, a) - v_\pi(s)$$

Where:
*   $Q_\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{l=0}^\infty \gamma^l R(s_{t+l}) \mid s_t = s, a_t = a \right]$ is the action-value function.
*   $v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi} [Q_\pi(s, a)]$ is the state-value function.

#### Numerical Intuition (From Whiteboard Example)
Consider an agent in state $s_t$ where the current policy $\pi$ has a state-value $v_\pi(s_t) = 50$. Assume the agent can choose from four possible actions $\{a_0, a_1, a_2, a_3\}$ with the following expected cumulative rewards ($Q$-values):
*   $Q_\pi(s_t, a_0) = 50$ (Default action chosen by current policy)
*   $Q_\pi(s_t, a_1) = 30$
*   $Q_\pi(s_t, a_2) = 40$
*   $Q_\pi(s_t, a_3) = 60$

Calculating the Advantage $A_\pi(s_t, a_i) = Q_\pi(s_t, a_i) - v_\pi(s_t)$:
*   $A_\pi(s_t, a_0) = 50 - 50 = 0$
*   $A_\pi(s_t, a_1) = 30 - 50 = -20$
*   $A_\pi(s_t, a_2) = 40 - 50 = -10$
*   $A_\pi(s_t, a_3) = 60 - 50 = +10$

```
          State s_t (v_π = 50)
         /      |      \      \
     a_1/    a_2|    a_0|    a_3\
       /        |       |        \
  Q=30     Q=40     Q=50     Q=60
  A=-20    A=-10    A=0      A=+10
```

*   **Interpretation:** The sign of the advantage function indicates whether taking action $a$ is superior ($> 0$) or inferior ($< 0$) to the policy's average performance. Action $a_3$ shows positive advantage, indicating a clear scope for policy improvement by shifting probability towards $a_3$.

---

### 2.3. Estimating the Advantage
The simplest way to estimate the advantage function in policy gradient algorithms is to approximate the action-value $Q_\pi(s_t, a_t)$ using a single-step Temporal Difference (TD) target:

$$Q_\pi(s_t, a_t) \approx R(s_t) + \gamma v_\pi(s_{t+1})$$

Substituting this back into the advantage formulation yields:

$$A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \approx R(s_t) + \gamma v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - v_\pi(s_t)$$

*   **Bias-Variance Trade-off:** While a 1-step TD estimate has low variance, it is highly biased because it relies on the current estimate of $v_\pi(s_{t+1})$. To address this, more robust estimators like **Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE)** are used in practice, which combine multi-step bootstrapping targets to balance bias and variance.

## 3. Policy Comparison: Derivation from First Principles

Our core objective is to express the performance of a new policy $\pi'$ directly in terms of the performance of our old policy $\pi$.

### Theorem: Policy Performance Difference Identity
For any pair of policies $\pi'$ and $\pi$, the difference in their performance can be written exactly as:

$$\eta(\pi') = \eta(\pi) + \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right]$$

---

### Step-by-Step Proof
Let $\tau = (s_0, a_0, s_1, a_1, \dots)$ be a trajectory sampled under the new policy $\pi'$. We begin by expressing the expected value of the sum of discounted advantages of the old policy $\pi$ along this new trajectory:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right]$$

Using the 1-step definition of the advantage function, we write:

$$A_\pi(s_t, a_t) = \mathbb{E}_{s_{t+1} \sim P} \left[ R(s_t) + \gamma v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - v_\pi(s_t) \right]$$

Taking the expectation of the discounted sum of these advantages over the entire trajectory $\tau \sim \pi'$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t \left( R(s_t) + \gamma v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - v_\pi(s_t) \right) \right]$$

Let us expand the summation term:

$$\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t \left( R(s_t) + \gamma v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - v_\pi(s_t) \right) = \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R(s_t) + \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^{t+1} v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t v_\pi(s_t)$$

Now, let us examine the relationship between the last two summation terms. Writing out their terms explicitly:

$$\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^{t+1} v_\pi(s_{t+1}) = \gamma v_\pi(s_1) + \gamma^2 v_\pi(s_2) + \gamma^3 v_\pi(s_3) + \dots$$

$$\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t v_\pi(s_t) = v_\pi(s_0) + \gamma v_\pi(s_1) + \gamma^2 v_\pi(s_2) + \gamma^3 v_\pi(s_3) + \dots$$

Subtracting the second sum from the first reveals a **telescoping sum** where all intermediate terms cancel:

$$\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^{t+1} v_\pi(s_{t+1}) - \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t v_\pi(s_t) = -v_\pi(s_0)$$

*(Note: We assume $\lim_{t \to \infty} \gamma^t v_\pi(s_t) = 0$, which holds since $\gamma < 1$ and the reward space is bounded).*

Substituting this back into our expanded expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R(s_t) - v_\pi(s_0) \right]$$

Since the expectation is linear, we split the terms:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R(s_t) \right] - \mathbb{E}_{s_0 \sim \pi'} \left[ v_\pi(s_0) \right]$$

By definition:
1.  $\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t R(s_t) \right] = \eta(\pi')$
2.  The initial state $s_0$ is drawn from a starting distribution $p(s_0)$ which is independent of the policy. Thus, $\mathbb{E}_{s_0 \sim \pi'} [v_\pi(s_0)] = \mathbb{E}_{s_0 \sim \pi} [v_\pi(s_0)] = \eta(\pi)$.

Substituting these definitions yields:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] = \eta(\pi') - \eta(\pi)$$

Rearranging terms proves the identity:

$$\eta(\pi') = \eta(\pi) + \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] \quad \text{(Equation 1)}$$

## 4. Constructing the Surrogate Objective

### 4.1. The Distribution Shift Problem
While Equation 1 is mathematically exact, it is practically unusable for optimization. The expectation is taken over trajectories $\tau \sim \pi'$ (the new, modified policy):

$$\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi'} \left[ \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t A_\pi(s_t, a_t) \right] = \sum_{t=0}^\infty \sum_s P(s_t = s \mid \pi') \sum_a \pi'(a \mid s) \gamma^t A_\pi(s, a)$$

To evaluate this expectation, we need to know the state visitation probabilities under the *new* policy $\pi'$. However, during optimization, we do not yet have trajectories from $\pi'$—we only have data collected using our old policy $\pi$.

```
Old Policy π (Known data):  s_0 -> s_1 -> s_2 -> s_3 (Green Path)
New Policy π' (Unknown):    s_0 -> s'_1 -> s'_2 -> s'_3 (Red Path)
```

---

### 4.2. Discounted State Visitation Frequency
To simplify the notation, we define the discounted state visitation frequency under a policy $\beta$:

$$\rho_\beta(s) = \sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t P(s_t = s \mid \beta)$$

Using this definition, we rewrite the exact performance difference:

$$\eta(\pi') = \eta(\pi) + \sum_s \rho_{\pi'}(s) \sum_a \pi'(a \mid s) A_\pi(s, a)$$

---

### 4.3. The Surrogate Objective Formulation ($L_\pi$)
To resolve the dependency on $\rho_{\pi'}(s)$, we make a local approximation: we replace the state visitation frequency of the new policy $\rho_{\pi'}(s)$ with that of the old policy $\rho_\pi(s)$. This defines the **surrogate objective** $L_\pi(\pi')$:

$$L_\pi(\pi') = \eta(\pi) + \sum_s \rho_\pi(s) \sum_a \pi'(a \mid s) A_\pi(s, a)$$

*   **Key Property:** At the current policy parameter point where $\pi' = \pi$, the surrogate objective matches the true objective in both value and first-order derivative:
    $$L_\pi(\pi) = \eta(\pi)$$
    $$\nabla_{\theta'} L_\pi(\pi') \vert_{\pi'=\pi} = \nabla_{\theta'} \eta(\pi') \vert_{\pi'=\pi}$$

---

## 5. Theoretical Guarantees: Monotonic Policy Improvement

How far can we update $\pi'$ away from $\pi$ before the approximation $\rho_{\pi'}(s) \approx \rho_\pi(s)$ breaks down and degrades performance? Schulman et al. established a lower bound on the true policy performance.

### Theorem 1 (TRPO Policy Bound)
The true performance of a policy $\pi'$ is bounded below by its surrogate performance minus a penalty term proportional to the divergence between the policies:

$$\eta(\pi') \ge L_\pi(\pi') - C D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi, \pi')$$

Where:
*   $D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi, \pi') = \max_s D_{KL}(\pi(\cdot \mid s) \parallel \pi'(\cdot \mid s))$ is the maximum KL divergence over all states.
*   $C = \frac{4 \epsilon \gamma}{(1 - \gamma)^2}$ is a penalty coefficient.
*   $\epsilon = \max_{s, a} \vert A_\pi(s, a) \vert$ is the maximum absolute advantage.

---

### Monotonic Improvement Proof Sketch
Let our lower bound profile be defined as:

$$M(\pi') = L_\pi(\pi') - C D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi, \pi')$$

At our current point $\pi' = \pi$, we know:
$$D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi, \pi) = 0 \implies M(\pi) = L_\pi(\pi) = \eta(\pi)$$

If we choose our next policy $\pi_{new}$ by maximizing this lower bound:

$$\pi_{new} = \arg\max_{\pi'} M(\pi')$$

Then it must hold that:

$$\eta(\pi_{new}) \ge M(\pi_{new}) \ge M(\pi_{old}) = \eta(\pi_{old})$$

This inequality guarantees **monotonic policy improvement**: every optimization step of the lower bound is guaranteed to improve (or keep equal) the true policy performance.

```
Performance Value
       ^
       |          True Policy Performance η(π')
       |                 _--~--_
       |               _-       -_
       |             _-           -_
       |            /               \
       |           |   Surrogate     \
       |           |   Objective      \
       |           |   L_π(π')         \
       |          / .------_            \
       |         / /        -_           \
       |        | /           -_          \
       |_______|/_______________-__________\______> Policy Space (θ)
               |  \               -_
               |   \  Lower Bound   -_
               |    \ M(π')           -_
              π_old  π_new
```

## 6. The Trust Region and Constrained Optimization

### 6.1. Transitioning from Penalty to Constraint
In practical tasks, the penalty constant $C = \frac{4 \epsilon \gamma}{(1 - \gamma)^2}$ is extremely large because the discount factor $\gamma$ is typically close to $1$ (e.g., $0.99$). This causes the penalty term to dominate, restricting the policy to incredibly small, computationally impractical step sizes.

To allow larger, robust policy updates, TRPO reframes the penalty-based lower bound optimization as a **constrained optimization problem** over a "trust region":

$$\max_{\pi'} L_\pi(\pi') \quad \text{subject to} \quad D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi, \pi') \le \delta$$

Where $\delta > 0$ defines the radius of our trust region. 

This formulation guarantees that the new policy remains within a neighborhood where the surrogate approximation $L_\pi$ is accurate, avoiding catastrophic policy collapse.

---

### 6.2. Geometric Parameter Space Intuition
We can visualize this optimization constraint in the policy parameter space $\theta$:

```
               θ_2 ^
                   |             Vanilla Gradient Step
                   |                  \
                   |                   \  /|
                   |                    \/ |
                   |                    /  \
                   |                   /    \
                   |             .----/--.   \
                   |            /    /    \   \
                   |           /    /      \
                   |          |    o--------======> TRPO Step (Within Trust Region)
                   |          |  θ_old     |
                   |           \          /
                   |            \    δ   /
                   |             '------'
                   |_________________________________> θ_1
```

*   **Vanilla Policy Gradient:** Takes a step in the direction of the policy gradient. If the step size is too large, the policy can update to parameters that produce highly divergent actions, leading to a permanent drop in performance.
*   **TRPO:** Constrains the parameter update step to lie strictly within the KL divergence boundary $\delta$. It deforms the update geometry using the Natural Gradient (via the Fisher Information Matrix) to ensure the step size is safely measured in the space of policy behavior rather than parameter values.

---

## 7. Mathematical Formulation of the TRPO Algorithm

Putting all the components together, the formal TRPO optimization loop is executed as follows:

1.  **Initialize** the policy parameters $\theta_0$.
2.  For iteration $i = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ until convergence:
    *   Collect trajectories using the current policy $\pi_{\theta_i}$.
    *   Estimate the advantage values $A_{\pi_{\theta_i}}(s_t, a_t)$ for all sampled state-action pairs.
    *   Solve the following constrained optimization problem to find the next policy parameters $\theta_{i+1}$:

$$\theta_{i+1} = \arg\max_\theta \sum_s \rho_{\pi_{\theta_i}}(s) \sum_a \pi_\theta(a \mid s) A_{\pi_{\theta_i}}(s, a) \quad \text{subject to} \quad \bar{D}_{KL}^{\rho_{\pi_{\theta_i}}}(\theta_i, \theta) \le \delta$$

Where the average KL divergence is defined as:

$$\bar{D}_{KL}^\rho(\theta_i, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \rho} \left[ D_{KL}(\pi_{\theta_i}(\cdot \mid s) \parallel \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s)) \right]$$
